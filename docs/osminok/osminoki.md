# The Osminoki

## Biology

### Basics

**Body Plan:** Carbon-based cephalopodaform with bilateral symmetry. 2 humanoid arms/hands with 10 webbed fingers. 8 lower sucker-covered tentacles in place of legs. 2 pelvic fins, 1 dorsal fin, 1 adipose fin. 6 abdominal gills. 1 highly-sensitive cranial mantle where humanoid hair would be. 1 pair of ear-fins used primarily for expression and communication. Ventral-origin mouth with regenerating mineral teeth and relatively weak cartilaginous jaws. Teeth are a mineralization byproduct: excess calcium and iron from a shell-heavy diet get shunted into continuously replacing tooth rows, a metabolic overflow that happened to make eating more of those organisms easier.

**Physical Scale:** Adults range from roughly 2–5m (~6–16.5 ft.) at full tentacle extension, with females averaging larger than males (an adaptation for egg defense during the Vigil). However, this maximum height is rarely their *presented* height — tentacles constantly adjust, coil, and redistribute, making effective stature variable moment to moment. Land-based locomotion resembles the Earth octopus's slap-and-drag: tentacles grip and pull the body forward in a rolling, undulating gait. An Osminoki "walking" at a comfortable pace averages roughly humanoid height. Gaby, for instance, is on the small side at ~8 feet tall fully extended, but averages around 5'8" in practice. The cartilaginous skeleton makes them bouncy and squishable; they easily absorb impacts that would shatter bones and laugh it off.

**Circulatory System:** Copper-based hemocyanin blood (vivid blue when oxygenated). Three hearts: two branchial (smaller, for gill circulation) and one systemic (larger, for body circulation). Countercurrent heat exchangers (*retia mirabilia*) in major blood vessels trap metabolic heat in the brain, core organs, and tentacle nerve ganglia, maintaining elevated internal temperatures while the skin and gills stay near ambient (see Thermoregulation). Less efficient oxygen transport in warm environments — hemocyanin binds oxygen within a narrow optimal temperature band, and when the ambient-to-core thermal gradient shrinks, the whole system underperforms. Completely functional in 0-G.

**Oral Anatomy:** The mouth is ventral-origin, positioned slightly lower on the face than a human mouth, framed by soft, flexible lips that seal tightly for underwater pressure management and lots of facial expressions. The jaws are cartilaginous and relatively weak; Osminoki are crushers and grinders rather than biters, working food against their continuously regenerating tooth rows with repetitive lateral motion.

The tongue is a thick, muscular organ anchored farther back in the oral cavity than a human tongue. It has perfectly good range of motion relative to where it sits, but the anchor point means the tip cannot comfortably reach the front teeth or the alveolar ridge. In Noki speech, this is irrelevant; the tongue shapes water-jet pulses by partially occluding the oral cavity to modulate pressure, rhythm, and resonance, and it does this well. In air-breathing languages, the posterior anchor makes any phoneme requiring tongue-tip-to-front-teeth contact (dentals, alveolar fricatives, alveolar trills) come out slushy or substituted. This is the source of the lateral lisp. Osminoki can learn to speak Galactic Basic and most other languages clearly enough to be understood; they cannot learn to speak it without the lisp, and most of them have stopped trying as a point of mild collective pride.

The dorsal surface is covered in dense backward-facing micro-papillae, rougher than a human tongue, for gripping slippery food and positioning it between tooth rows. The ventral surface is smoother. The entire surface is packed with chemoreceptors at higher density than even the suckers, making the tongue the final quality-control checkpoint before swallowing. The suckers do the initial taste-test out in the world; the tongue does the "actually, *is* this going to kill me?" pass at the threshold. Given that off-world Osminoki eat essentially anything that was once alive, including organisms from biospheres their ancestors never encountered, a paranoid tongue is good engineering.

**Nervous System:** Approximately 60% of an Osminoki's neurons are located in the tentacles rather than the central brain, distributed across eight semi-autonomous nerve cords with their own local processing, motor programs, and sensory integration. Tentacles problem-solve semi-independently and often act on curiosity or tactile interest while the conscious mind focuses elsewhere.

The central brain sits in the cranium, protected by a hefty cartilaginous braincase. It is organized into distinct processing regions rather than the relatively uniform tissue of a mammalian cortex:

The optic lobes are the largest structures, taking up more cranial real estate than the rest of the brain combined. Osminoki vision is complex enough to demand serious dedicated hardware. Behind the optic lobes sits the cognitive core: the memory and learning complex, structured as a dense fan-out network where a relatively small number of input fibers distribute across millions of tiny interneurons, each forming an independent association site. This architecture makes Osminoki *very* fast associative learners; they build connections between concepts quickly and across many simultaneous channels, which is partially why immersion-based language acquisition works so well for them.

The motor command centers handle the brain's relationship with the tentacles, and that relationship is more managerial than direct. The brain doesn't map to specific tentacles; it can't say "move tentacle three over there and grasp the object," it says "grab the thing" and whichever tentacles are best positioned figure out the biomechanics. The primary brain represents actions, not body parts. Below the motor centers sit the chromatophore control lobes: hundreds of thousands of dedicated motor neurons whose axons project directly to the skin. Given that chromatophore signaling serves as both camouflage and language, the Osminoki brain devotes roughly as much neural tissue to controlling skin color as most sapient species devote to controlling their vocal apparatus. A separate and terrifically complex region processes electromagnetic field input from the cranial mantle.

Suckers on their tentacles contain chemoreceptors functioning as finely-tuned contact sensors, allowing Osminoki to "taste" any surface they touch or slither across. Each sucker runs its own local reflex loop: accepting food (yum), rejecting non-food (yuck), adjusting grip, and sampling chemical gradients without consulting the brain at all.

A chemical signal in Osminoki skin passively inhibits sucker adhesion on contact, which prevents tentacles from accidentally gripping each other or sticking to a neighbor during crowded social situations. The brain never has to compute avoidance; the suckers handle it autonomously. This reflex is easily overridden by conscious intent. Osminoki hold hands and tentacles just fine when they want to.

**Molecular Adaptation:** Rather than evolving separate genes for different environmental conditions, the Osminoki genome employs a trick: it edits its own neural RNA after copying it from the DNA but before building proteins from it. The editing responds to environmental conditions, especially temperature. An Osminoki in cold water is running measurably different protein variants in its nervous system than the same individual would in warm water, without a single gene having changed. The scope is enormous; the majority of neural RNA is subject to this rewriting, compared to a negligible fraction in most other species.

This is the mechanism that makes one species, four body plans possible without speciation. The polar giant, the vent garden dweller, the Twilight Shelf generalist, and the deep-ocean variant aren't diverging genetically. Think of it as the same genetic manuscript annotated differently depending on where the reader grows up. A fry developing in polar water gets a different set of margin notes than one developing in a vent garden, and the body builds itself accordingly.

**Skeletal System:** Cartilaginous endoskeleton (no bones, just teeth). Highly compressible and flexible; can squeeze through any opening the ribcage equivalent fits through (~8–10cm diameter when fully compressed). The lack of rigid skeletal structure also makes the entire body a pressure sensor. Osminoki feel compression changes through their cartilage and skin simultaneously: the approach of a large organism displacing water, a pressure wave from a distant cave collapse, the subtle gradient shift of descending ten meters. On Osminok, where sonar-capable predators and tectonic activity are constant threats, this whole-body barometric sense is just as important as vision.

**Integumentary System:** The skin is a layered optical instrument. Five distinct cell types and structures contribute to the Osminoki's ability to change color, texture, and luminosity on the ~~fly~~ swim:

1. **Chromatophores (outermost layer):** Multicellular pigment organs consisting of a central pigment sac surrounded by a ring of radial muscle fibers. When the muscles contract, they stretch the sac from a pinpoint dot into a flat disk, dramatically increasing its visible area. When the muscles relax, elastic recoil snaps the pigment back to a point in a spandec. The pigments are ommochromes, a single molecular family that produces yellow, orange-red, or brown-black depending on its chemical state and concentration. Within this layer, lighter chromatophores (yellow) sit closer to the surface than darker ones (red, then brown-black). Chromatophore control is entirely neural, not hormonal, which is why it's so goddamn fast.
2. **Iridophores (middle layer):** Cells containing stacks of thin protein plates that act as biological mirrors, producing iridescent blues, greens, golds, and silvers through structural optics rather than pigment chemistry. The reflected color depends on plate spacing and thickness; thicker stacks reflect warm colors, thinner ones reflect cool. Because reflectance changes with viewing angle, iridophores produce the characteristic shimmer that makes Osminoki skin catch light the way oil on water does. Iridophores also polarize the light they reflect, a property Osminoki can see but most other species cannot, creating a private optical channel for communication.
3. **Leucophores (deepest passive layer):** Broadband white reflectors containing thousands of tiny microspheres that scatter all wavelengths equally in all directions. In the ocean, where ambient light is filtered toward blue-green at depth, leucophores passively reflect whatever spectrum reaches them, providing automatic environmental color-matching without active input.
4. **Photophores** (distributed, primarily in deep-ocean and polar variants): Bioluminescent organs capable of self-generated light production. In deep-ocean Osminoki, where ambient light is nonexistent, photophores replace chromatophore-based communication almost entirely. These organs modulate color, intensity, pattern, and rhythm with a precision the shallower variants don't need and couldn't match. Two deep-ocean Osminoki in full bioluminescent conversation is a true spectacle: patterns of light blooming and chasing each other across the body, pulsing against total darkness like a signal fire that keeps changing its mind about what color to be.
5. **Papillae (texture layer, distributed across the skin surface):** Muscular dermal protrusions that erect and retract through pure hydrostat action, the same no-skeleton, all-muscle principle that drives the tentacles. Papillae transform skin from glass-smooth to rough, bumpy, spiky, or ridged in under a ribec. They are controlled through a separate neural pathway from chromatophores (routed through a peripheral ganglion rather than the brain's chromatophore lobes), meaning texture and color adjust simultaneously and independently. A camouflaging Osminoki matches the grain, pitting, and crevice shadows of a rock or coral surface in real time, not just its color. Papillae also serve a communicative function: certain texture states carry social meaning (bristled = agitated, glass-smooth = calm or deliberately presenting, rough-ridged = defensive, etc.), and experienced Osminoki read texture as fluently as they read color.

These five systems interact in combination. Expanded chromatophores over iridophores act as colored spectral filters; yellow pigment over blue iridescence produces green, for instance. Retracted chromatophores reveal the lovely iridophore shimmer beneath. Chromatophores over leucophores appear as brighter, more saturated versions of their pigment color. Fully retracted chromatophores with exposed leucophores produce brilliant white (best seen on the polar variants). Papillae adjust the 3D surface that all of this plays across. The result is access to virtually any color, brightness, contrast, pattern, and physical texture in the visible spectrum, despite possessing only warm-colored pigments and some anxiety.

The skin also has a limited capacity for light detection independent of the eyes. Photosensitive proteins in chromatophore-associated cells respond to light, particularly in the blue range, causing chromatophores to expand without brain input. This gives the skin a low-resolution ambient light sense: an awareness of local brightness that feeds into camouflage reflexes and, in deep-ocean variants, allows the body surface to faintly register incoming bioluminescent signals. Conversation between deep-ocean Osminoki is a full-body experience, sending with the photophores, receiving with the eyes and the skin simultaneously.

Their default relaxed state is usually smooth, with default coloration depending upon the phenotype.

### Fight & Flight

**Camouflage & Display:** Each camouflage attempt is an active pattern-match search. The skin iterates through candidate patterns, adjusting and readjusting until it converges on something that matches the environment. The process takes a few ribecs and usually looks like subtle rippling as intermediate states blur past. A calm, experienced Osminoki converges quickly. A stressed one may overshoot, oscillate, or settle on a pattern and then fidget out of it. Stress visibly degrades camouflage quality and is one of the most reliable external indicators that an Osminoki is having a real bad time.

The chromatophore control system is lateralized: each side of the brain predominantly controls the chromatophores on its own side of the body. This allows an Osminoki to display two different patterns simultaneously, one on each flank. The social applications are predictable: showing agreement to one conversational partner while signaling discomfort or annoyance to another, running a courtship display on one side while projecting a threat pattern at a romantic rival on the other, or simply expressing genuine ambivalence by letting each half of the body say something different. Doing this deliberately is considered an advanced social skill. Doing it accidentally, broadcasting conflicting signals without realizing it, is a common source of embarrassment and a dead giveaway that someone is lying or torn. Maintaining a consistent pattern across both flanks while feeling something contradictory takes effort. An Osminoki poker face is a whole-body discipline.

**Defense Mechanisms:** Produces clouds of bitter ink when startled or threatened. Ink contains mild sensory-dulling compounds. Tentacles regenerate if severed (~1 rigon), and may continue autonomous movement briefly after separation.

**Healing & Resilience:** Osminoki heal extraordinarily fast. Minor cuts and scrapes clot within martrons and fully close within a couple of marbecs. They can survive nearly any injury short of complete bifurcation or shredding, including traumatic head injuries — the distributed nervous system means no single wound site can take out enough neurons to be immediately fatal. Hemocyanin blood clots rapidly on contact with open water. The species has some traditional remedies derived from hunted or gathered organisms, but their bodies are largely self-repairing. Mental illness, however, is taken more seriously, as psychological distress directly affects tentacle function (imagine half your limbs experiencing depression while the other half don't). Elder Osminoki with extensive life experience typically help the younger ones handle mental health issues on a case-by-case basis. The species is prone to anxiety disorders.

### Senses

**Vision:** Osminoki are tetrachromats. Four photoreceptor types cover a spectral range wider than human vision at both ends: near-UV (~350–400nm), blue-green (~450–520nm), orange-red (~590–650nm), and near-IR (~700–780nm). The weighting is biased toward the spectral extremes, exactly the wavelengths that matter in an ocean with almost no sunlight, where ambient light is filtered to blue-green and everything else is bioluminescence. They resolve fine color distinctions across their full range, which is why their communication is so chromatically dense.

Compared to a human, an Osminoki sees more at the edges and somewhat less in the yellow-green middle. A field of wildflowers on a sunny terrestrial world looks muted and oddly flat to an Osminoki. A pitch-black ocean trench full of bioluminescent organisms looks like a cathedral at golden marbec.

Visual temporal resolution is high. Osminoki eyes resolve rapid flicker that would blur together for a human observer, necessary for reading sub-ribec chromatophore display changes at conversational speed.

**Polarization:** Osminoki eyes detect the polarization of light, the orientation of the light wave itself, which is invisible to humans and most other sapient species. Polarized vision cuts through underwater scatter and haze, reveals transparent or glass-like organisms that would otherwise be invisible, and opens a private visual channel. Iridophores polarize the light they reflect, which means polarization-modulated skin signals are readable by other Osminoki but undetectable to nearly everyone else. Two Osminoki can hold a side conversation in polarized iridophore flicker while standing in a room full of humans who see nothing but a faint shimmer.

**Electromagnetic Field Sense:** The cranial mantle is the Osminoki's most distinctive sensory organ. Positioned at the body's highest point, it detects and generates electromagnetic fields across a range that overlaps with the bioelectric signatures produced by living nervous systems while also looking fabulous.

*Passively,* an Osminoki can sense the presence, approximate size, and emotional state of nearby organisms without seeing them. Every nervous system generates a weak EM field; the mantle reads these the way an ear reads sound. Sensitivity sits at roughly 1–2 nanovolts per centimeter, sufficient to detect a human-sized nervous system at about 4–5 meters in open water or a large predator at 10 meters or more. Fine emotional detail, the difference between "calm" and "anxious" in a nearby person, resolves at about 1–2 meters. Stressed creatures produce different field signatures than calm ones, injured ones different from healthy ones. The sense works through most non-metallic barriers (composite bulkheads, organic walls, rock) with reduced range; conductive metals block it almost entirely.

*Actively,* Osminoki can modulate their own EM field output through the mantle. This is the basis of EM-field communication: a subliminal broadcast of emotional state and intent that other Osminoki pick up as a felt sense rather than a decoded signal. "Vibes" is the closest Galactic Basic gets to describing it. EM modulation adds an emotional underscore to spoken and visual language; an Osminoki saying "I'm fine" while broadcasting distress on the EM channel is fooling nobody whose mantle is working.

At higher intensities and with trained precision, coordinated EM field output becomes "singing," the harmonic modulation that Void-Singer Choirs use to communicate with Starsingers. The Starsingers' nervous systems respond to specific EM frequency patterns the way a tuning fork responds to its resonant pitch. When a dozen Osminoki harmonize their EM output into a coherent directional signal, they can trigger and guide a Starsinger's jump reflex. This is how Teuthida first communed with the Singers, and ***this organ is the sole reason the Osminoki have FTL travel.*** No other (documented) species in the galaxy navigates by singing electromagnetic lullabies at a giant colonial organism until it folds spacetime. The Osminoki do it because their sensory hardware evolved for reading the mood of a Darkmaw at fifty meters, and it turned out the same hardware could also talk to something much, *much* more important.

**Chemoreception (Smell/Taste):** In water, Osminoki chemoreception is devastating. Waterborne sensitivity to amino acids and hemocyanin/hemoglobin breakdown products reaches approximately 1 part per 10 billion. In practical terms, a wounded organism bleeding into open water is detectable at roughly 200–300 meters with a favorable current, or about 40–50 meters in still water. An Osminoki doesn't need to see prey to track it; they follow the chemical gradient through the water like reading a trail of footprints, tasting the trail getting stronger with every meter. The suckers provide extreme close-range resolution on top of this, fine enough to identify individual organisms, assess food quality, and read chemical traces left on surfaces.

In air, this entire system falls apart. Without a liquid medium to dissolve and carry molecules to the receptors, airborne "smell" is functionally nonexistent. An Osminoki on a space station cannot smell dinner cooking down the corridor, has no idea why everyone just wrinkled their noses after that *brrrrtt* noise, and would have to physically touch the source of any airborne scent to identify it. This is a genuine sensory gap in non-aquatic environments and a persistent minor frustration for Osminoki in the diaspora. The phrase "I'll take your word for it" gets a lot of use around scented candles, perfume, and open sewage alike.

**The Full Package:** Tetrachromacy, polarization vision, passive skin-light-sensing, EM field detection, whole-body pressure sensitivity, and waterborne chemoreception. An Osminoki stalking prey on the Twilight Shelves is reading the scene across a dozen channels simultaneously: four color bands, polarization angle, EM field signatures from nearby nervous systems, compression waves from movement, chemical traces through the suckers, and ambient light levels through the skin itself. Exhausting, isn't it?

### Environmental Interactions

**Major Environmental Adaptations:** Fully amphibious. Excellent swimmers. Gills for underwater respiration, mouth/nose otherwise (still using interior gill structures for oxygen extraction). Withstands extreme atmospheric pressure (1,000+ bars). Immune to nitrogen narcosis and decompression sickness. Omnivorous with strong carnivorous preference. High metabolism requiring frequent meals; most of the caloric budget goes to maintaining core temperature in near-freezing water (see Thermoregulation).

**Thermoregulation:** Mesothermic. Osminoki maintain elevated temperatures in critical tissues — brain, cranial mantle, core viscera, systemic heart, and tentacle nerve ganglia — through countercurrent heat exchange, while the skin, gills, and peripheral tissues remain near ambient water temperature. They are warm inside and cold to the touch; a handshake with an Osminoki is chilly, but cuddling one might be nice.

The warm brain ensures fast cognition and reaction times regardless of water temperature. The warm cranial mantle enhances EM field sensitivity by reducing thermal noise. The warm tentacle ganglia keep the distributed nervous system responsive; sixty percent of an Osminoki's neurons would be sluggish and stupid without their own local heating, which is not a good look for a species that relies on its tentacles to independently dodge things trying to eat it.

This comes at a cost. Maintaining a thermal gradient against near-freezing water is calorically expensive, and most of an Osminoki's food intake goes to fueling internal heat production rather than growth or activity. This is the real driver behind the high metabolism and frequent feeding requirement: they aren't just active, they're running a submersible furnace. In warm environments, the cost reverses; the ambient-to-core gradient shrinks, excess heat can't be dumped efficiently, blood oxygen transport degrades, and the Osminoki overheats.

**Special Needs:** Must fully submerge in water for at least 4 marbecs per malton unit. Can survive by routinely dousing if submersion is impossible, but this is less efficient and uncomfortable.

**Sleep:** Unihemispheric; half the brain sleeps while the other half and the tentacles remain alert. They can swim and sleep simultaneously. The sleeping hemisphere drops temperature slightly, saving energy while the waking hemisphere maintains full warmth and processing speed. True full-body sleep only occurs a few times per nargon, when they feel genuinely safe; this coincides with a controlled whole-body temperature dip, a brief, shallow torpor that lets the thermal system reset. Water is a much better thermal buffer than air, so sleeping submerged means a slower, more predictable cooldown. An uncontrolled cooldown in dry air is uncomfortable at best and dangerous at worst, which is one more reason sleeping underwater is greatly preferred.

Full-body sleep has two stages. Quiet sleep comes first: pale, still, chromatophores mostly retracted. Then, roughly every marbec, a bout of active sleep lasting about 10–15 martrons. During active sleep the skin comes alive. Chromatophores fire in rapid sequences, cycling through the same patterns the Osminoki uses while awake: camouflage textures, social signals, threat displays, bioluminescent pulses in the deep-ocean variants. Tentacles twitch. Ear-fins flutter. There's lots of involuntary squelching.

This is ***dream-broadcasting***. It is involuntary, uncontrollable, and to anyone who (somehow) knows how to read Osminoki body language, a raw, unfiltered window into whatever the sleeper is processing. Sleeping near someone is an act of trust. On Osminok, where true full-body sleep requires feeling safe, dream-broadcasting is usually visible only to denmates, Fastfriends, and romantic partners.

To everyone else in the galaxy (AKA nearly everyone) a sleeping Osminoki just looks beautiful. Slow ripples of color and texture moving across the body, patterns that seem to mean something but never quite resolve into anything a non-Osminoki observer can parse. The deep-ocean variants are particularly striking; a Osminoki from the trenches in full-body sleep in a dark room is a living lava lamp, glowing and shifting with bioluminescent pulses that track the emotional rhythm of a dream nobody else in the room can parse.

This makes Osminoki surprisingly popular bunkmates as long as nobody needs the lights off.

### The Fun Stuff

**Diet (Off-World):** Meat whenever possible, and they're extremely not picky about the source. High-fiber foods are difficult for them to process, but they'll try just about anything that was once alive. They prefer soft foods due to their weak cartilaginous jaws, and enjoy enrichment with meals — cracking open shells with tentacles, drizzling sauces and syrups onto things before eating, that sort of thing. Their regenerating teeth handle tough textures fine; it's the jaw strength that limits them.

**Intoxicants:** Osminoki are extreme lightweights with alcohol. Ethanol acts as a rapid-onset depressant on cephalopod nervous systems — a drink or two that would mildly buzz a human can flatten an Osminoki, though they recover quickly once they stop. On Osminok, Slimespirals secrete a psychoactive mucus with psilocybin-like effects (euphoria, heightened social bonding, vivid color-shifting), and Osminoki serotonin pathways are similar enough to mammalian ones that a wide range of serotonergic substances work on them. Getting turnt when you aren't *absolutely certain* you're safe is a death sentence on Osminok, so recreational use always involves a group, a defensible location, a trip-sitter, and a Fastfriend for good vibes.

## Evolutionary History

The Osminoki didn't arrive at their current body plan overnight, and they certainly didn't do it on purpose. What follows is the Abbey's best reconstruction, assembled from Memory-Stone deep-archives, comparative anatomy with other cephalopodaforms, and a healthy amount of educated guessing by xenobiologists who keep publishing papers with titles like *"No, Seriously, How Did They Get Hands?"*

### The Ancestor

The starting point is a small benthic cephalopodaform: ten undifferentiated limbs, a ventral cartilaginous mouth, lateral eyes, a muscular mantle for jet propulsion, and the full cephalopod starter pack of chromatophores, distributed nervous system, and the good sense to hide when threatened. Body orientation is horizontal. Size is modest, maybe 15–60 cm. Habitat is the shallow kelp forests of the Twilight Shelves, where the organism clings to fronds, hides and camouflages among the canopy, and hunts opportunistically on whatever it can catch or scavenge.

This ancestor is also, very probably, similar to what Facefish look like now. The resemblance isn't coincidence and it isn't mimicry. Facefish are living snapshots of the body plan the Osminoki left behind: horizontal, generalized, still clinging to the kelp, still watching with those big forward-facing eyes. The oldest and most universal hunting taboo on the planet makes a lot more sense when you realize the Osminoki aren't refusing to eat something that merely looks like them, but represents what they used to be.

### The Vertical Shift

The forests of the Twilight Shelves are three-dimensional environments. Fronds grow 10–40 meters tall, forming dense vertical canopies that sway with the current. A horizontal organism clinging to a flat rock is adapted for a two-dimensional world. An organism clinging to a kelp stipe inhabits a vertical one — lower limbs gripping the frond, upper limbs reaching into the canopy to snag passing prey, body stretched along a swaying, flexing substrate that demands constant dynamic balance and recoloration.

Over deep time, this vertical lifestyle drove the first great divergence. The lower limbs specialized for anchoring: stronger grip, broader sucker surfaces, more flexible bases for absorbing the kelp's constant movement. The upper limbs specialized for exploration and prey capture: finer motor control, higher chemoreceptor density at the tips, faster reflexes for grabbing small targets drifting through the fronds. The body elongated along the vertical axis, because a taller reach on a vertical surface means more food access without releasing your grip. The mantle — which in the horizontal ancestor sat behind and above the head — rotated to sit on top of the head in the vertical posture, repositioning the EM-sensitive tissue at the body's highest point: the best vantage for detecting electromagnetic fields from all directions, especially from above, where the nastiest surprises tend to come from.

This is the fork. Facefish stayed horizontal, kept all ten limbs generalized, and retained the ancestral body plan. The proto-Osminoki went vertical and never went back.

### Going Up & Out

Tsunari has been driving tidal ranges of 40–100 meters for billions of cycles. On Osminok, the intertidal zone — the band of rock and substrate that cycles between submerged and exposed twice per malton unit — is hundreds of vertical meters wide, and anything sessile or slow-moving in it is stranded and vulnerable at low tide. The proto-Osminoki hauled themselves between tide pools to hunt trapped prey, tolerating brief air exposure for the caloric payoff, and Osminok's atmosphere made it possible. The 2.5 bar pressure means a decent amount of oxygen per breath, and 30–40% water vapor means the air itself is practically soup. Breathing dense, humid, oxygen-rich air through a wet gill membrane is a dramatically more forgiving challenge than doing it on a dry world.

Over millions of cycles, the external gill filaments, which were fragile in air and prone to collapse and desiccation, migrated inward, becoming increasingly enclosed in protective body cavities that retained moisture and maintained gas exchange in both water and atmosphere. The six abdominal gills never stopped being gills, they just got a roof. The modern 4-marbec submersion requirement is the maintenance cost: re-hydrating membranes, flushing metabolic waste, and letting the gill tissue run at full aquatic capacity to clear all the yucky particulates that air-breathing accumulates. Dousing works in a pinch. A proper soak works better.

The irony is that the megastorms made the whole adaptation nearly pointless at home. The surface that selected for amphibious capability became a permanent kill zone of hurricane-force winds, Stormskate hunting runs, and waves that rip tentacles off rocks. For roughly hundreds of thousands of cycles, the Osminoki have been carrying fully functional air-breathing hardware with almost no on-world reason to use it. A few niche applications remain — brief surface excursions during storm breaks, reaching the island beacons, pilgrimages to Threshold Crag — but these are cultural uses of a biological trait whose original purpose no longer exists.

And then they left the planet, and suddenly the single most important line on an Osminoki's resume is that they can breathe air. A fully aquatic species can't mop a spaceship floor. The trait that Osminok's megastorms rendered obsolete is exactly what makes them employable off-world. Evolution didn't build them for space, it built them for snacking. Space just happened to be hiring for the same skill set.

### Getting Hands

Earth cephalopods already show limb specialization — octopuses use their front arms differently from their rear ones, and squid have two dedicated tentacles distinct from their eight arms. "Some limbs do different jobs" is baseline cephalopod biology. The Osminoki's upper manipulatory limbs simply took that baseline and swam with it.

The driver is dexterity in a complex three-dimensional environment. Reaching between kelp fronds, snagging small fast-moving prey in a tangle of vegetation, manipulating caught food while maintaining grip with the lower limbs — all of this rewards finer control at the tips. The evolutionary pathway is tip-bifurcation; branching at the arm-tips, first a simple fork, then further subdivision over geological time, each branch gaining independent motor control courtesy of a nervous system that already runs most of its processing locally. Webbing between the branches is retained (useful for swimming, cupping small prey, creating sealed grips around slippery targets), but the branches themselves become progressively more articulate. Ten digits across two arms is a lot by vertebrate standards, but for a species with dedicated nerve clusters driving each finger-analog independently, the motor control architecture was already in place.

Stingplant exploitation enters the picture later as a powerful reinforcer. Once proto-digits already exist from kelp-canopy life, the ability to reach through a forest of nematocyst-loaded tentacles and extract those tasty, calorically-dense Stingfish eggs from the base of a sessile predator rewards precision even further. The Stingplant immunity co-evolves alongside the digit refinement because individuals with the best hands *and* the highest nematocyst tolerance accessed an exclusive food source nothing else on the planet could touch. Two independent traits under correlated selection pressure, feeding back on each other across thousands of generations. One could call that *pretty handy!*

### The Mouth, Teeth, & Jaws

The ancestral ventral mouth — cartilaginous, bottom-facing, built for scraping biofilms and soft prey off kelp surfaces — never needed to be powerful. The vertical posture gradually repositioned it to face forward, but the structure remained fundamentally the same: a flexible cartilaginous frame suited to an organism that increasingly solved the food problem with its hands and tentacles rather than its bite. Their tongue just checks that whatever they're about to swallow isn't going to kill them, and can push food back out on its own before it touches anything important. This is considered entertaining by Osminoki onlookers.

The teeth are a secondary development and, like many of the best evolutionary innovations, started as waste management. Osminok's food web is saturated with hard-shelled organisms whose calcium carbonate exoskeletons and iron-rich mineral content must go somewhere once digested. Without bones to remodel, the excess minerals needed an outlet. Continuously regenerating tooth rows — grown, used, shed, and replaced — became a mineralization dump that happened to improve the organism's ability to grip and tear soft prey. Which in turn made it easier to eat more of the shelled organisms producing the mineral surplus. Which grew more teeth. A feedback loop, not a single innovation: the teeth didn't evolve *for* eating hard things, they evolved *from* eating hard things, and then made eating them easier.

The jaws stayed weak because they never needed to be particularly strong. An Osminoki that can crack a Crunchmeat shell with their tentacles, shuck a Siftshell with their fingers, and peel apart a Crunchclaw with ten independently articulated digits doesn't require a powerful bite. The mouth receives food the hands have already processed. It's a secondary tool and always has been.

### The Spawning Migration

For most of their evolutionary history, the proto-Osminoki spawned in the shallows. Before the megastorms, the upper ocean was warmer — likely 8–15°C in sheltered kelp forest pockets — and the egg-layer could guard her clutch wrapped around a kelp stipe in the canopy, surrounded by the convenient cover that the species already called home. Fry hatched into the richest biome on the planet with immediate access to Smallplant films, Meatmeat clouds, and the relative safety of the fronds.

Then the megastorms began.

Roughly 200,000–400,000 cycles ago, whatever happened to Tsunari destabilized the planet's climate. Cloud albedo skyrocketed. Surface temperatures crashed. The intertidal zone became overtly hostile. The upper ocean dropped from a range where egg incubation was reliable to one where it wasn't; embryos developing too slowly, failing to reach critical thresholds, or dying outright in water that had become just a few degrees too cold.

The species faced a reproductive bottleneck, and the individuals who survived it were the ones who went looking for warmth. Hydrothermal vents still output water at hundreds of degrees, and the gradient zones around them maintain stable elevated temperatures continuously regardless of what insane shit the surface is doing. Spawning near vents meant the eggs developed properly. It also meant descending thousands of meters into unfamiliar, predator-dense territory that the ancestral kelp-dweller had no experience navigating.

This deep spawning migration reshaped the species. Pressure tolerance, enhanced bioluminescence for navigation in total darkness, EM field sensitivity for detecting threats without light, the refined cold endurance needed for transit through the frigid Midnight Plains — all of these became critical traits because they had to survive the deep long enough to reproduce. The Vigil at depth amplified every selection pressure: the egg-layer needed to last a full cycle in near-freezing vent-margin water, defending her clutch against predators she'd never evolved alongside, burning through caloric reserves to maintain both her body and the mesothermic furnace keeping her brain fast enough to react.

Ventral energy storage depots — the lipid-dense reserves positioned over the core organs (the ones that look like mammalian breasts) — were driven by Vigil survival. Egg-layers with larger reserves guarded longer, maintained cognitive function deeper into the cycle, and produced fry from better-defended clutches. Sexual selection reinforced the trait: egg-fertilizers assessing potential partners had a visible, reliable indicator of metabolic health and Vigil-readiness right there on the torso. The energy reserves became a signal as well as a survival tool, the same way plumage quality signals fitness in birds. Males and intersex individuals carry smaller baseline versions (energy storage is always useful), making the size differential a secondary sex indicator in a species that otherwise looks fairly similar across reproductive roles.

### The Sensory Crown

The cranial mantle in its modern form is the ancestral mantle relocated and repurposed. Positioned at the body's highest point, it places the EM-sensitive tissue where it can detect fields from all directions — critical for an organism whose worst predators can approach from anywhere. In mesothermic Osminoki, the mantle runs warmer than ambient water, and the resulting difference enhances electromagnetic sensitivity.

The ear-fins are derived from lateral fins that originally served a secondary locomotory function in the horizontal ancestor. The vertical posture made head-mounted swimming fins less useful, but the tissue was already richly supplied with chromatophores and positioned right next to the face — exactly where another Osminoki is already looking during social interaction. Over time, the fins broadened and became primarily communicative: flared for alarm, folded for submission, spread for display, angled for nuance. On a planet where sound attracts predators, having a large, mobile, silent signaling surface on your face keeps you alive. They are, functionally, the eyebrows of the species, but with a much higher communication bandwidth.

### What Stayed the Same

For everything that changed, the Osminoki never lost the core cephalopodaform toolkit. Chromatophores for rapid color change, a distributed nervous system that runs the majority of its processing in the limbs, jet propulsion for emergency escape, ink for defense, a cartilaginous skeleton that compresses, flexes, and squeezes through anything, and the ability to taste the world through contact.

The body plan evolved around these features rather than replacing them. Every adaptation layers on top of the ancestral architecture without dismantling it. An Osminoki with all of its derived traits stripped away would still be recognizable as a cephalopodaform. It would also look a lot like a Facefish. With boobs. Blech.

The cephalopodaform lineage on Osminok is old beyond meaningful reckoning, predating anything the Memory-Stones can reach. The proto-Osminoki divergence — the vertical shift, the hands, the whole body plan that makes them recognizable — is millions or billions of cycles deep, far older than recorded history and far older than the Teuthidan Abbey (estimates range between 800 million–1.2 billion cycles). The modern species in its current form, with its deep spawning migration, its variant populations, and its full suite of social, pressure, and cold adaptations, is a product of the megastorms and is therefore probably somewhere between 200,000 and 400,000 cycles young.

The Osminoki don't know any of this in precise terms because their fossil record is functionally nonexistent. Cartilage doesn't fossilize, and they don't even have archaeologists on Osminok. Why bother? Memory-Stones only go back as far as someone was alive to encode them. The Abbey's deepest archives reach back to Teuthida's era, which means their entire recorded history covers roughly 7–14% of the time since the megastorms began. Everything before that is inference, comparative anatomy, and the occasional ancient Memory-Stone pulled from a dead organism that happened to have one growing inside it when it died millions of cycles ago — tantalizing, fragmentary, and impossible to date with any precision.

They know they're old. They don't know *how* old. They have no idea they're still mid-metamorphosis.

## Phenotypic Variants

Different ocean biomes have produced distinct Osminoki populations with recognizable physical differences. These are not separate subspecies — any two variants can interbreed — but reflect long-term adaptation to local conditions.

What keeps them a single species despite significant phenotypic divergence is the deep-vent spawning bottleneck. Every variant, regardless of home biome, descends to hydrothermal vents to reproduce. The vents are communal spawning grounds shared across all populations: shelf-dwelling, deep-ocean, vent garden, and polar Osminoki all breed in the same waters, and their fry scatter into the same post-hatching chaos. A broodguard scooping up survivors doesn't sort by phenotype. A polar giant's eggs incubate next to a deep-ocean miniature's eggs at the same vent. The fry mingle, the genomes remix, and the variants stay connected.

It is a species held together by its own inevitable death. Every breeding event remixes the gene pool because the parents all die in the same place.

### Twilight Shelf Osminoki (Baseline)

**Size:** 2.5–4m | **Build:** Mid-range, canopy-optimized | **Coloration:** Muted greenish-blue | **Distribution:** Most abundant

Twilight Shelf Osminoki are the baseline, mostly living similarly to their mutual ancestor. The kelp forests of the Twilight Shelves are where the species evolved, and the Shelf population is what you get when nothing pushes it to change further. Mid-range size is optimized for navigating dense frond canopy without getting tangled. Muted greenish-blue coloration blends into the bioluminescent kelp environment. Camouflage specialization is the strongest of any variant, reflecting a habitat where visual concealment in complex cover is the primary survival strategy.

They receive the widest genetic input of any variant population, because fry from every vent site on the planet can end up in a kelp forest. A Twilight Shelf Osminoki may carry polar gigantism alleles, deep-ocean bioluminescence genes, or vent-garden heat tolerance traits that never express because the environment doesn't trigger them — but the alleles are there, dormant, ready to activate if that individual's fry happen to end up somewhere colder, deeper, or hotter.

### Deep-Ocean Osminoki (Gaby's Variant)

**Size:** 2–3m | **Build:** Smaller, compact, crevice-adapted | **Coloration:** Pallid purple-blue (default), most versatile bioluminescent display capability | **Distribution:** Uncommon

The deep-ocean variant is probably the youngest branch on the Osminoki tree, and its origin is accidental.

Broodguard triage at the vent spawning sites is fast, brutal, and imprecise — scooping handfuls (later netfuls) of tiny fry out of a feeding frenzy and swimming hard for safety. Some fry get missed. Some scatter in the wrong direction. Some end up wedged into trench wall crevices near the vent sites and simply... chill. The Crushing Deep is lethal for an adult caught in open water, but a 2 cm juvenile that can compress itself into an 8 mm crack in a basalt wall is functionally invisible to everything large enough to be a threat. The vents provide warmth, the crevices provide shelter, the local ecosystem provides food that scales up as the juvenile grows.

The ones who survived this were the ones small enough to keep exploiting crevice-and-crack architecture into adulthood. The deep-ocean variant trends smaller because a compact body navigating tight trench wall spaces avoids the open-water exposure that kills larger individuals at depth. You don't need to outrun a Darkmaw if you can slip into a fissure it can't follow you through.

Bioluminescent versatility followed directly from the absence of all other light. The Twilight Shelves get faint illumination from above; the Crushing Deep gets none. Communication, navigation, hunting, courtship — everything that uses chromatophore displays in the shallows needs a self-generated light source at depth, and the only light source available is one's own body. Deep-ocean Osminoki didn't just get brighter, they got *more articulate;* capable of modulating color, intensity, pattern, and rhythm with a precision the shallower variants envy but simply don't need.

The pallid purple-blue default coloration is an energy-saving measure. Why maintain expensive baseline chromatophore activity for camouflage matching when your default environment is pitch black? The pale resting state conserves metabolic resources, and when they *do* activate chromatophores, the contrast against that pale base makes every display even more visible. It's the same tradeoff cave-adapted organisms make when they lose pigmentation, except the Osminoki replaced passive camouflage with active signals that would make any raver jealous.

### Vent Garden Osminoki

**Size:** 2.4–3.7m | **Build:** Larger, heavier, heat-tolerant | **Coloration:** Deep reds and blacks (mineral staining) | **Distribution:** Moderate, clustered around vent sites

These are the ones who decided the spawning commute was pointless and just moved in permanently.

The baseline lifecycle involves descending to the vents to spawn and then (via broodguard) the fry returning to the shallows to grow up. The vent garden variant represents a population that cut out the return trip entirely. Why go back to the kelp forests when the vent gardens have warmth, mineral-rich water, dense food webs, and you're already sitting on top of the spawning site?

The tradeoff is that vent gardens are *harsh as hell.* Temperatures swing from near-freezing ambient to superheated vent output across meters. Geyser eruptions are unpredictable and can scald anything nearby. The mineral-heavy water and diet load the body with iron, sulfur, and heavy metals. Predator density runs high because the entire local food web is concentrated around the vents.

So the vent garden variant got bigger and harder. Larger body mass provides thermal inertia; a big body changes temperature more slowly than a small one, buffering the wild thermal swings near active vents. Rougher default skin texture is likely mineral deposition in the integument itself, the same process that produces their characteristically dark red-and-black coloration: not a pigment change, but a patina. The chromatophores are still there underneath, but the epidermis is stained by a lifetime of iron sulfide and metal compound exposure.

Vent garden Osminoki also benefit from a thermoregulatory advantage. Living in the warmest sustained-temperature habitat on the planet means the mesothermic gradient between core and environment is smaller, which means less caloric expenditure on internal heating. They can afford to build bigger, denser bodies not because they eat more than their Twilight Shelf cousins, but because more of what they eat goes to growth and muscle rather than running the furnace. Same engine, lower heating bill, more energy left over for everything else. "Often the strongest individuals in a region" isn't a boast, it's thermodynamics, baby.

They are also, almost certainly, the population that invented the broodguard system. Vent garden residents live permanently at the spawning sites. They watch every clutch laid, every hatching frenzy, every wave of fry scattering into the dark. They know the local predator patterns, the safest escape routes, the timing of geyser eruptions that clear the area of threats. The broodguard tradition didn't appear fully formed as a species-wide institution, it started as vent garden residents protecting the clutches in their own backyard, and the practice spread outward as other communities recognized its value and sent their own volunteers to learn.

Genetically, the vent garden variant is probably the most internally consistent. They never leave the spawning zone. They breed where they live. Their gene pool is smaller and more localized than the Twilight Shelf population, which receives fry from every vent site on the planet. These folks just hang out.

### Polar Osminoki

**Size:** 3.5–5m | **Build:** Massive, slow-growing, thermally optimized | **Coloration:** Pale, almost translucent (minimal chromatophore activation at rest) | **Distribution:** Rarest, polar ice shelves only

The largest, rarest, and most thermodynamically committed variant. Also the hardest to explain, because getting to the poles requires crossing the single scariest biome on the planet.

The Twilight Shelves are temperate. The vent gardens are deep. The poles are *really far away,* separated from both by the vast, exposed, cover-free Midnight Plains — the most predator-accessible hunting ground on Osminok, where anything visible is a target and anything glowing is bait. No sane adult Osminoki crosses the Plains voluntarily, and the distance from the nearest vent spawning sites to the polar ice margins spans thousands of kilometers of open water. Not a commute so much as an odyssey. So how did they get there? They drifted!

Osminok's powerful Coriolis forces and Tsunari-driven tidal currents create strong meridional circulation, pole-to-equator and back. Fry that scatter from high-latitude vent sites can get caught in poleward currents and carried to the ice shelf margins before they're large enough to swim against the flow. Most die en route. A 2–3 cm juvenile in open water is food for virtually everything. But cephalopodaforms are already cold-tolerant, the fry are small enough to avoid the attention of the big scary open-water predators, and the survivors who arrive at the ice margin find something extraordinary: a habitat that is rich, concentrated, defensible, and mostly devoid of competitors.

The ice shelves pack life into tight vertical layers between the shallow seafloor and the ice ceiling. Nutrient upwelling along the ice margins feeds some of the densest biomass on the planet. Temperatures are brutal but constant, with none of the wild thermal swings of the vent gardens, none of the seasonal flux of the Twilight Shelves. For a mesothermic organism, stable cold is easier to manage than variable cold, because the countercurrent heat exchangers can calibrate to a consistent gradient and stay there. Once established, the gigantism feedback loop is self-reinforcing, driven by the single most important equation in thermoregulation (say it with me now): *surface area to volume ratio!*

A larger body has proportionally less surface area relative to its volume. Less surface area = less heat loss per unit of body mass. In near-freezing water, this is the difference between a sustainable caloric budget and burning through food faster than you can catch it. A small Osminoki at the poles would spend most of its caloric intake just keeping warm, leaving little for growth, activity, or reproduction. A large one retains core heat far more efficiently, spending a smaller fraction of each meal on the furnace and banking the rest into fat and muscle tissue. Each generation that grows slightly larger has slightly better thermal economics, and over thousands of cycles, body size ratchets upward.

Their 4–5 meter size range is a classic case of polar gigantism. Earth's colossal squid reaches 10+ meters in Antarctic waters under the same pressures, and that's a conventional cephalopod without the Osminoki's mesothermic advantages. A 5-meter Osminoki under the ice is big, but it's exactly the size the physics says it should be.

The pale, near-translucent coloration serves two functions simultaneously, both driven by the unique light environment under the ice. Looking down from the ice ceiling, a translucent body blends with the faintly bioluminescent water column below. Looking up from beneath, a pale body disappears against the ice above; it's countershading taken to its logical extreme. But it also saves energy! Fewer active chromatophores in the skin means less metabolically active tissue in the integument, which means less heat bleeding through the skin surface. Every chromatophore that ain't firing is a tiny reduction in thermal loss. The polar variant traded camouflage flexibility for thermal efficiency — they *can* still color-shift, but the range is narrower and the resting state is near-zero pigment activation. They are saving every fraction of a calorie, even the ones hiding in their skin.

Their solitary behavior is a direct consequence of size rather than temperament. The Osminoki's ancestral survival strategy is group stealth — small clusters using chromatophores, texture mimicry, and coordinated movement to vanish into the environment. At 4–5 meters, you don't vanish easily. Two polar Osminoki together in open water are a conspicuous target in a way that two Twilight Shelf individuals simply aren't. So the polar variant shifted strategies entirely to solo camouflage (translucence against ice) and the raw deterrent of being goddamn enormous. A solitary polar Osminoki pressed flat against an ice ceiling is nearly invisible. A pair of them is either dinner or death, depending on your size & temperament.

This is also why the polar Fastfriend bond is the strongest and most codependent of any variant. A solitary apex that can't safely group with its own kind still benefits enormously from a hunting partner that doesn't occupy the same stealth niche. Polar Fastfriends 5 meters long, built for wrestling oversized prey against the ice ceiling, and the partnership is genuine mutual dependence. The Osminoki provides tactical intelligence, ambush planning, and the manipulative dexterity to handle prey the Fastfriend's conical teeth can't process alone. The Fastfriend provides speed, bite force, and the thermoregulatory comfort of a warm-blooded body to sleep curled against in freezing water. A spear-wielding Osminoki riding a polar Fastfriend at full tilt under the ice, all tentacles locked around the dorsal ridges while the Fastfriend zooms after prey — this is the closest thing the polar variant has to the group coordination their smaller kin enjoy, compressed into a partnership of two.

Polar Osminoki consider this method of hunting the highest form of entertainment. They are not wrong.

### One Species, Four Answers

All four variants share the same fundamental body plan, thermoregulatory system, nervous system architecture, and reproductive biology. They are one species in four costumes, shaped by four different answers to the same question every organism on Osminok must eventually face: *where is it safest to be small, cold, and delicious?*

Variants primarily differ in adult size range, body density and musculature, default coloration and chromatophore investment, integumentary texture, bioluminescent capability and complexity, and the balance between caloric expenditure on thermoregulation versus growth. A cunning Twilight Shelf generalist, a diminutive deep-ocean crevice specialist, a beefy vent garden bruiser, and a polar giant blessed in the chest can all meet at the same spawning vent, all produce viable offspring together, and all die in the same water while their fry scatter into the dark carrying every variant's genes all jumbled together. The ocean sorts them out from there.

## Lifecycle & Reproduction

**Sexual maturity:** ~20 cycles
**Prime breeding age:** 25–40 cycles
**Maximum lifespan:** 100–150+ cycles if not killed. The 50–70 cycle average lifespan on Osminok is entirely due to predation and their breeding method, not biological limits.

**Osminoki are semelparous:** They breed once and die. Most avoid it as long as possible. Mating typically occurs when an individual is already weakened/injured, when resources are abundant, or when a compatible partner is encountered.

**Process Overview:**

- Brief courtship (color displays, tentacle gestures, EM field harmonics)
- Squiggly wiggly internal fertilization (details best left to the imagination)
- Female lays ~100 fertilized eggs near a hydrothermal spring (elevated water temperature promotes healthy development) and guards them
- Once they hatch, the parents die and the surviving offspring are raised by others

**Fertilization:** Here are the details anyway. Internal, via a modified lower tentacle (the gonopod) that transfers spermatophore packets to a seminal receptacle near the egg-laying orifice at the crux of the receiving partner's tentacle mass. The process requires extensive lower-tentacle intertwining and precise positioning — both partners must actively cooperate, which is probably for the best given what they're agreeing to. Intersex individuals possess both gonopod and receptacle; hormonal shifts during courtship determine which role they take in a given pairing. Fertilization usually occurs several malton units before egg-laying, and the female stores the spermatophores until she reaches a suitable vent site. By the time the eggs are laid and attached to the warm substrate, the hormonal cascade that will eventually kill both parents has already begun.

**The Vigil:** The mother guards her clutch for ~1 cycle (~3 rigons). She does not leave and does not eat. 100% of her energy goes to protecting the eggs. Her body consumes itself: fat, muscles, organs, eventually brain function. By hatching time, she is emaciated, barely conscious, and running on pure instinct.

**The Hatching:** ~100 eggs hatch simultaneously via chemical trigger. Juveniles ("fry") are 2–3cm long, fully formed but tiny and translucent. They immediately scatter in all directions. 90–95% are eaten within martrons (by everything, including each other). The mother watches them scatter. Seeing her young burst from their eggs is her release: this triggers the automatic death response, and she dies within marbecs. Her body becomes a nutritious distraction for nearby scavengers.

**Fertilizer's Death:** After fertilization, a hormonal cascade begins. The partner who fertilized the eggs slowly breaks down over the same cycle-long period. Usually dies from predation as the body weakens, starvation, or simple biological shutdown. Rarely, if ever, sees the offspring.

**The Broodguard:** Egg-laying vents are communal gathering points, shared across phenotypic communities. Each spawning site is watched over by a rotating cadre of broodguards — the strongest and most cunning warriors the community can spare. Their sacred duty is non-interference until the last possible moment: when nearby predators begin descending on the hatching frenzy, the broodguards sweep in with finely-woven mesh bags, scoop up as many surviving fry as they can carry, and swim hard for safety. It's brutal triage — they can't save everyone, and they don't try. They save what they can.

**Communal Rearing:** Since Osminoki only ever produce one clutch, any and all surviving fry are raised communally by whoever will take them. The community feeds, shelters, and teaches them. It's very much "the village raises the child." Language, survival skills, cultural knowledge, and (if near the Abbey) religious teachings are all passed down by unrelated adults. Hatchlings grow to roughly half their adult size within 5–6 cycles, reaching full size around 12–15 cycles. Because they're fiercely independent by nature, some young Osminoki split off to go solo after only a handful of cycles. Most who do are never seen again.

**Reproduction off-world:** Biologically possible, practically catastrophic. An Osminoki clutch of ~100 eggs needs sustained warmth (vent-margin temperatures, roughly 10–20°C at the egg site), a saline aquatic environment, and a full cycle of uninterrupted guarding. A sufficiently motivated pair could theoretically rig a heated saltwater tank in a ship's engine bay or a station's industrial section and carry the Vigil to term. Some almost certainly have.

The problem is what happens next. One hundred hatchlings need food, water, space, and supervision. Both parents are dead. There are no broodguards, no vent garden community, no communal rearing network — just a space station that now has a hundred tiny, frightened, ravenous cephalopodaforms loose in the ventilation system. On Osminok, 90–95% mortality in the first minutes is the norm, and the survivors are caught and raised by a society built for exactly this. Off-world, that mortality rate is either replicated by neglect (which is monstrous) or avoided by preparation (which requires a support network most spacefaring Osminoki simply don't have).

The Abbey's informal guidance to those leaving via beacon is blunt: *Don't breed off-world unless you have already built the village that will raise the children.* Most spacefaring Osminoki, being intelligent and acutely aware of what reproduction costs, take this advice seriously. Same-sex relationships remain the primary avenue for companionship without deadly consequence, and the extremely rare off-world breeding events that do occur tend to involve established Osminoki communities on water-rich stations where enough adults have pooled resources to approximate the communal rearing system. These are planned, collective decisions — never accidents, never casual. You don't get to be casual about the thing that kills you after you do it once.

### Fry Development

Osminoki hatchlings are 2–3 cm, nearly fully formed in miniature, translucent, and operating almost entirely on instinct. They hatch with ten undifferentiated limbs — no arm/tentacle distinction, no digit bifurcation, no specialized gonopod. The upper pair won't begin visibly differentiating for several cycles. A freshly-hatched Osminoki looks, in fact, very much like a tiny Facefish, which has not escaped the Abbey's notice.

**First marbecs (the Scatter):** Fry disperse in all directions on pure reflex. Chromatophores are present but limited to simple transparency shifting — enough to be hard to see, not enough to mimic anything complex. Bioluminescence is absent. EM sensitivity is minimal. The fry navigate by pressure gradients, chemical traces, and vibration. They eat Smallplant films, Smallmeat, and each other.

**First nargon (~1 month):** Survivors have grown to 5–8 cm. Chromatophores begin producing basic color changes: alarm flashing, crude camouflage matching, and the first clumsy attempts at social signaling. The upper limb pair starts to thicken and diverge from the lower eight, though the tips remain unbranched. Fry at this stage cling to kelp, rocks, or each other in loose aggregations for mutual predator detection. They are beginning to learn what they are, mostly by watching anything nearby that moves.

**1–3 cycles (~6–18 months):** Rapid growth to 15–40 cm. Digit bifurcation begins at the upper limb tips — first a simple fork, then progressive branching over several cycles. The webbing fills in between the emerging digits as they separate. Bioluminescence comes online, initially as involuntary flickers in response to stress or excitement (juvenile Osminoki are notorious for lighting up when startled, which is counterproductive but very endearing). EM sensitivity develops gradually; young Osminoki describe it as a "buzzing" that slowly resolves into directional information. Ear-fins are present but small, mostly used for balance rather than communication. The mesothermic system is functional but inefficient — juveniles run colder than adults, tire faster, and need to eat almost constantly. They're growing, heating, and learning to use new senses all at the same time, and the caloric demand is ferocious. Communal rearing groups spend a disproportionate amount of their effort simply keeping the young ones fed and more or less in one place.

**3–8 cycles (~1.5–4 years):** Growth to roughly half adult size. The full digit complement is present and increasingly dexterous, though fine motor control continues developing for several more cycles — a 4-cycle juvenile can grip, tear, and grab, but precision tasks like Stingplant egg extraction or Memory-Stone encoding are still clumsy. Chromatophore and bioluminescent control reaches near-adult sophistication. EM sensing matures. This is the stage where personality, curiosity, and individual temperament become unmistakable, and where culturally transmitted skills — language fluency, hunting technique, navigation, religious teaching if near the Abbey — are absorbed fastest. The mimicry reflex is at its most aggressive: young Osminoki at this stage unconsciously copy everything and everyone around them, picking up behavioral patterns, color habits, and movement styles from adults, other juveniles, companion species, and occasionally inanimate objects they've decided are interesting. This is also the stage where some juveniles split off to go solo, driven by the species' deeply-rooted (if inadvisable) desire for independence.

**8–15 cycles (~4–7.5 years):** Full body size reached. Digit dexterity, chromatophore control, bioluminescent range, and EM sensitivity all at adult levels. Mesothermic efficiency reaches its mature baseline. The ear-fins reach their full adult size and communicative range. Sexual maturity is still several cycles away, but the body is otherwise complete. An Osminoki at 15 cycles is physically an adult in everything except reproductive capability and, arguably, good judgment.

**~20 cycles (~10 years):** Sexual maturity. The gonopod or receptacle (or both) completes development. The body is now capable of reproduction, but "capable" and "ready" are very different things on Osminok. Most individuals wait — often for dozens and dozens of cycles — because the decision to breed is also the decision to die, and dying at 20 cycles when you could live to 100+ feels like poor planning.

### Senescence

Osminoki can live well beyond 100 cycles if nothing eats them and they don't breed. Few reach this threshold — on Osminok, predation claims most individuals long before biology does, and the drive to reproduce eventually wins out over the drive to survive. But those who do grow old present a portrait of a body in slow, uneven retreat.

The mesothermic system degrades first in almost all cases. The countercurrent heat exchangers lose efficiency as the vascular tissue stiffens with mineral deposits; the same calcium and iron that built their teeth all their lives now slowly accumulates in places it shouldn't. Core temperature drops, fraction of a degree by fraction of a degree, over dozens of cycles. The elder gets colder. Meals that once sustained a full thermal cycle now fall short. They eat more, they slow down, or both.

The healing factor — the species' most celebrated resilience — is the next casualty. Wounds that once closed in marbecs take many malton units. Severed tentacle tips that once regrew in a rigon take two or three, or don't fully regenerate at all. The rapid clotting that kept hemocyanin blood from attracting every predator in the ocean becomes sluggish. An aging Osminoki accumulates scars for the first time in their life, and each one is a visible record of declining regeneration.

What happens to the nervous system is where it gets complicated, and where the distributed architecture makes Osminoki aging genuinely unlike anything in the vertebrate experience.

The central brain and the tentacle ganglia age at different rates. The central brain, which is warm, well-perfused, metabolically prioritized, tends to hold its function longer. The tentacle ganglia, dependent on their local heating and further from the core blood supply, cool and slow first. An aging Osminoki may notice their tentacles becoming "stubborn"; slower to respond, less creative in their independent problem-solving, more likely to repeat familiar patterns rather than discovering novel solutions. The experience is not painful. It's more like the tentacles are getting tired. Tasks that once required no conscious thought — opening containers, navigating tight spaces, the constant sensory intake from tentacles independently investigating — start requiring deliberate attention from the central brain. The elder compensates by thinking harder about things they used to do automatically, which is cognitively exhausting.

In some individuals, however, the progression reverses. The tentacle ganglia remain sharp while the central brain degrades. This presents as something resembling dementia in its early stages, with confusion, memory gaps, personality changes, and difficulty with abstract reasoning. The body remains physically capable and the tentacles continue functioning with disconcerting competence. An Osminoki in this state can navigate familiar environments, hunt, eat, and defend themselves on tentacle-memory alone while their central cognition fractures. They are, in a sense, still *themselves* from the tentacles down. The person dissolves while the animal persists. This is considered the worse outcome by far. The Osminoki have a saying for it that translates roughly to "the arms remember what the mind forgets," and it is not said fondly.

Elder Osminoki who recognize the signs in themselves often choose to breed while they can still make the choice consciously, trading a diminished future for offspring. Others seek out the Abbey for brine preservation, wanting to be remembered as they were. Some simply swim into the depths and don't come back — whether this is a conscious final act of independent exploration in the spirit of the Fourth Tenet or something else, the Abbey does not speculate, at least not publicly.

## Gender, Romance, & Intimacy

Osminoki approach gender practically rather than ideologically. The two reproductive roles are egg-layer and egg-fertilizer; not "female" and "male" in any culturally loaded sense, just functional descriptions. Approximately 10% of Osminoki are intersex, capable of fulfilling either role, and are very highly valued as potential mates for their flexibility (though they still die ~1 cycle after breeding, same as anyone else).

Casual romantic relationships are almost nonexistent between compatible partners because the stakes are too high — you only get one clutch. If an Osminoki finds someone they consider a strong genetic and temperamental match, they pair up and stay together until conditions are safe enough to spawn, then say their farewells knowing what comes next. Same-sex relationships, carrying no reproductive consequences, are the primary avenue for romantic attachment without existential weight.

That said, Osminoki are a deeply touch-oriented species. Over a lifetime, an individual may have several entanglements (meant *very* literally... tentacle-twining, texture-sharing, EM field harmonization, even *handholding!*) with various partners, mostly as a way to test personality and genetic compatibility. On Osminok, these encounters are always conducted carefully: you only have one shot at continuing your line, and an accidental fertilization is a death sentence for both parties.

Osminoki don't care much for gender or orientation labels. By galactic classification standards, nearly all would register as pansexual — what matters to them is compatibility, not identity.

## Communication & Language

**Spoken Language (Noki):** Not vocalized in the traditional sense. True cephalopods are not known to vocalize intentionally; their primary communication is visual and tactile. Noki spoken language is a combination of:

1. **Water jet modulation:** Forcing water through siphon/mantle and mouth/nose in rhythmic pulses. Creates clicks, buzzes, rumbles in the 30–300 Hz range. Travels well underwater but is terrible in air, which is why Osminoki who learn Galactic Basic tend to speak it with a heavy lateral lisp.
2. **Oral clicks:** Sharp percussive elements (tongue clicks, teeth clacks) adding rhythm and emphasis. Function like consonants.
3. **Body percussion:** Tentacle slaps, fin claps, sucker pops. Used for emphasis or getting attention.

Spoken Noki sounds like rhythmic bubbling/clicking/rumbling with percussive accents. Completely unintelligible to air-breathers without underwater microphones and a lot of patience.

**Nonverbal Language (also Noki, equally important):**

1. **Chromatophore displays:** Color changes indicate mood, intent, social status. Rapid flashing = alarm/fear/excitement. Slow waves = calm/contemplation. Specific patterns = religious significance.
2. **Electromagnetic field modulation:** Generated and sensed via mantles. Used for navigation, emotional reading, "singing" to Starsingers. Subliminal communication transmitting intent/vibes.
3. **Tentacle gestures:** Specific curls, waves, and patterns adding meaning and emphasis. The distributed nervous system allows tentacles to convey parallel information: upper arms gesture one concept while lower tentacles gesture another. Creates incredibly dense communication, like sign language with 10 simultaneous channels, and can fully replace spoken language in most cases.

**Etymology:** "Noki" is the Osminoki word for communication itself. The species name breaks down as *osmi-* (to absorb, to take in through contact) + *noki* (communication) → "those who absorb communication." This reflects both their literal biology (chemoreceptive suckers that taste meaning from surfaces, chromatophores that passively mirror their surroundings) and their cultural identity as learners and mimics. The planet name "Osminok" drops the collective *-i* suffix: the world stands alone, a singular thing in the dark. The *-i* suffix is produced with the mouth open laterally wide and a sharp frontal water jet, physically embodying plurality and outward connection.

### Mimicry & Alien Language Acquisition

Everything about Osminoki biology is tuned for absorption. Their distributed nervous system processes sensory input across the entire body simultaneously; their chromatophores can reproduce virtually any color or pattern within ribecs; their skin texture shifts from glass-smooth to rough-scaled at will; their EM field sensitivity lets them read emotional states and social dynamics without understanding a single word being spoken. Put together, this makes the Osminoki exceptional mimics. An Osminoki immersed in an unfamiliar culture will unconsciously begin matching the body language, coloration, and social rhythms of those around them before they've consciously decided to learn anything. Pure reflex, the same instinct that lets a wild octopus vanish against a reef. A traveling Teuthidan Adherent can pick up the fidgeting habits of a Comexo cargo crew inside a malton unit without even trying.

Linguistic mimicry follows a similar curve. An Osminoki's mouth, siphon, and water-jet apparatus weren't built to produce the fricatives and plosives that air-breathing languages often rely on, so pronunciation is always an uphill fight. But *comprehension* moves fast. A few nargons of full immersion in a language group is enough to map most of the meaning: tone, cadence, repeated structures, emotional context from EM field reading, gestural patterns that speakers don't even realize they're producing. After roughly one cycle of sustained exposure, an Osminoki is conversational. After two, genuinely fluent. The lateral lisp never quite goes away, certain phonemes remain physically impossible (good luck getting an Osminoki to produce a voiced alveolar trill — their whole vocal apparatus is built for percussive bursts, not the kind of delicate sustained airflow that makes a tongue tip vibrate), and idioms built on alien biology sail right over their mantles. But they'll understand you, and you'll understand them, and they'll do it faster than almost any other species in the galaxy.

Call it functional echolalia rather than intelligence. The Osminoki nervous system evolved to process ten sensory channels at once on a planet where missing a single environmental cue meant death. The same hardware that lets an Osminoki taste a current shift and dodge a Razorback also lets them decode a trade pidgin over lunch.

#### A Note on Notes

Osminoki have no vocal cords, no larynx, and no vibrating membranes of any kind. They never evolved a mechanism for making air oscillate inside a tube because their ancestors never breathed air on purpose. When speaking air-breathing languages, they repurpose the same siphon-and-oral-cavity pressure system that drives Noki: forcing air through at controlled rates while the tongue shapes it. The sound source is turbulent airflow rather than a vibrating membrane. Percussive, plosive, and click-based phonemes are essentially native territory; Osminoki handle those as well as any air-breather.

Sustained vowels and voiced consonants are the hard part, since those rely on a constant vibrating tone underneath the shaped mouth sounds. An Osminoki approximates vowels with a sustained push of air through a narrowed aperture, producing something slightly raspy or hollow. Most dryskins can tell something about the voice sounds *off* beyond the lisp but can't pinpoint why. This is why. The engine underneath is completely different, and they're universally terrible singers because of it.

They also always sound a little wet. The oral cavity, siphon, and nasal passages are lined with mucous membranes that stay hydrated as a matter of biological necessity, and air moving across damp tissue picks up a faint slick quality that no amount of practice eliminates. It's subtle in casual conversation, more noticeable during sustained speech, and very obvious if they're dehydrated or stressed (both of which increase mucus production and happen with startling regularity). Most crewmates stop noticing after a few malton units. Some never stop noticing and are too polite to say so.

The net result is that Osminoki speaking Galactic Basic sound like very articulate, slightly damp beatboxers approximating a tonal language through pure pressure control and turbulence. They are, predictably, excellent actual beatboxers.

## Writing Systems

**1. Pressure Script (temporary, everyday):** Carved/pressed into soft sediment or Slowforest mats. Lasts marbecs to malton units. Geometric patterns optimized for being felt with chemoreceptors; read by tasting carved surfaces with tentacle suckers. Equivalent to chalk on pavement.

**2. Stone Script (permanent, formal):** Carved into basalt with harder stone tools. Lasts centuries but is labor-intensive. Angular, deeply cut characters to survive erosion. Used for sacred texts, historical records, Abbey architecture. Still readable by touch/taste. Equivalent to carved monuments.

**3. Crystal Script (archival, highest fidelity):** Encoded in non-implanted Memory-Stones using EM field patterns. An Osminoki "sings" to a seed crystal growing in mineral-rich solution, generating specific EM fields. The crystal grows around the field patterns, creating structural defects that preserve the electrical signature. Any other Osminoki can "replay" the stored pattern by touching it with their forehead or mantle. Information density is high (approximate equivalent of one copy of *The Fellowship of the Ring*, both book and movie, per finger-length crystal lattice), effectively infinitely retained, and the experience includes emotional and sensory context from the original encoder, not just abstract text. The Abbey's archives contain many thousands of Memory-Stones in sequential chains — and since each stone holds roughly one lifetime of experience, the chain itself is the Osminoki calendar. Teuthida's journey exists as a crystal-chain of dozens that tell the complete story with full sensory/emotional depth.

## Names & Identity

Osminoki names are multi-layered, multi-sensory communications that most landlubbers only perceive a fraction of. **Gabriola Vulgaris**, for example, has many parts.

**Gaby's full name incorporates:**

- **Sound component:** "GA-bree-OH-lah" (lateral lisp, rhythmic clicks), or "GA-bee" for short
- **Color component:** Rapid pulsing shift from deep purple to pale lavender and back ("soft-hearted"/"gentle-current")
- **Texture component:** Skin ripples smooth to slightly bumpy to smooth ("swift"/"flowing")
- **EM component:** Specific 42–68 Hz arpeggiated pulse pattern ("purposeful"/"determined")
- **Bioluminescence component:** Faint flicker along mantle edges ("rider"/"one who moves with intention")

**Full Translation:** *"The Soft But Determined One Who Rides Swiftly On Strong Currents With Gentle Purpose"*

Non-Osminoki perceive only the sound ("GA-bee") and maybe notice her color shifts without understanding the meaning. They get about 20% of her actual name.

"Vulgaris" is a bureaucratic tag (Neolatin for "common"), added by the first xenobiologist to catalog the species. Osminoki don't use surnames; they have no family structures persisting beyond a single generation.

## Society & Social Structure

**Total population:** ~5–8 million individuals planet-wide, spread across nearly the entire ocean at very low density.

**Social organization:** Mostly solitary (individuals or small groups of 3–8). Loose territorial home ranges. Temporary aggregations around resources (kelp forests, vent gardens, bone yards, reefs). Community is valued but not constant. The desire for a permanent group is somewhat unusual but not aberrant — cephalopods are curious, and Osminoki have genuine interest in others of their kind as well as aliens.

**Settlements:**

- **The Teuthidan Abbey** (largest): ~200 permanent residents plus dozens of pilgrims
- **Vent Communes** (~10–15 scattered): 30–50 residents each
- **Reef Clusters:** Temporary, form and dissolve seasonally

**Government:** None. The Abbey functions as de facto authority (religious + educational + archival), vent communes may have informal councils, but most Osminoki govern themselves and would have it no other way. No taxes, no laws, no enforcement beyond social and environmental pressure. Those with knowledge hold more social influence, but the ocean is the great equalizer; nobody can claim dominion when everything around them is trying to eat them.

**Conflict Resolution:** Osminoki strongly prefer settling disputes non-lethally. Confrontations typically begin with showy visual displays — rapid, precise color/pattern/texture shifting, bioluminescent flaring, fin and mantle spreading, fancy swimming; essentially a contest of who can put on the more impressive threat display. If displays don't settle it, disputes escalate to wrestling: a chaotic, full-tentacle, no-holds-barred grapple where the goal is to draw first blood. Once blood appears, the fight ends instantly since any lingering blood in the water attracts predators, making continued conflict suicidal for both parties. There are no bones to break, so injuries from these bouts are rarely serious and heal within marbecs.

**Taboos & Social Norms:** Most taboos are survival-based in origin, even if their practical roots have been forgotten. Hunting Nursemouths and Facefish is culturally forbidden. Interfering with another's food supply, compromising group safety, or disrupting reproduction are the most serious social offenses, punishable by shunning, which in a world this dangerous is effectively a death sentence. Privacy and personal ownership (beyond items currently being carried) aren't meaningful concepts; by human standards, Osminoki are rude and wildly invasive — they rummage through others' belongings freely, because communal access to resources is their default. Information and resources are shared openly with no expectation of repayment. The ocean giveth and the ocean taketh away. Are you gonna eat that?

**Crime:** Rare, mostly because the social structure doesn't incentivize it. When it occurs, it almost always involves the big three: food, safety, or reproduction. Theft as humans understand it barely exists — most things aren't "owned." The closest equivalent is hoarding: deliberately withholding resources others need. This is met with shunning and, in extreme cases, physical expulsion from a community.

**Consequences for Spacefaring Osminoki:** This communal default scales catastrophically off-world. Border control, permits, identification papers, legal jurisdiction, contractual obligation — none of these concepts map to anything in Osminoki experience. They routinely get in trouble because they literally don't understand why they're not allowed to go places or talk to certain people or take things they like.

## Everymalton Life

*"Don't die. Snack often. Explore a lot. Mate once. **Then** you die."*

That's the whole species in a clamshell. Here's the details:

### The Rhythm

Every living thing on Osminok dances to the same clock: the diel vertical migration. Smallplant blooms rise toward faint surface light; Smallmeat follows; Meatmeat chases the Smallmeat; predators chase everything. The ascending mass creates a density wave called the Shimmerlayer that propagates through the entire water column as pressure changes, chemical signatures, bioluminescence shifts, and EM field fluctuations that every Osminoki can sense to some degree, even at 7,000 meters.

The 18-marbec malton unit divides naturally around this wave:

| Marbecs | Phase | What's Happening |
|:-:|:-:|:--|
| 1–2 | Deep Lull | Everything at depth, quiet, settled. |
| 3–4 | The Stir | First upward movements begin. |
| 5–6 | Rising Hunt | Active ascent; prey bunched and disoriented. **Hunting window!** |
| 7–8 | Bright Drift | Prey near peak height, settling, alert. |
| 9–10 | High Water | Peak — everything at its shallowest, most visible, most cautious |
| 11–12 | The Turn | First downward movements begin. |
| 13–14 | Falling Hunt | Active descent; prey bunched and disoriented. **Hunting window!** |
| 15–16 | The Settling | Prey returning to depth, activity tapering. |
| 17–18 | Deep Rest | Approaching the lull — den time, sleep window. |

The two transition periods are the prime hunting windows. Prey is actively moving, bunched together, and briefly disoriented by the pressure and chemical changes accompanying the migration shift. Every Osminoki, from solitary polar giants to Abbey archivists, orients their lives around these two eternally-repeating events. Hunts are the social anchor of daily life the same way mealtimes structure a human household: the event everyone plans around, the thing you're either doing, preparing for, or recovering from.

Between proper hunts, the mesothermic furnace demands constant stoking. Osminoki graze opportunistically at all times — snatching Crunchclaws in passing, scraping Stingfish eggs from a convenient Stingplant, cracking open a Crunchsnack that happened to drift by. Their bodies do not accept excuses about meal timing. If something edible is within tentacle reach, it gets eaten. Saving food for later is a luxury reserved for dens with good storage; out in the open, calories consumed now are calories a scavenger can't steal from your corpse later.

Unihemispheric sleep means an Osminoki is almost always at least half-on, tentacles idly scouting nearby water even during rest. They still prefer returning to a den for the Deep Lull and Deep Rest — being half-asleep in open water is survivable, being half-asleep in a cave with a squeeze-entrance is much better. Full-body sleep, the proper torpor with its temperature dip and involuntary dream-broadcasting, only happens a few times per nargon and only in a secure underwater den. A nap in dry air is uncomfortable at best and dangerous at worst.

### The Living Space

Home is a curated ecosystem, not a built structure.

Osminoki live in natural formations: rock crevices, kelp forest canopy, voidcoral reef cavities, basalt cave systems; anywhere that offers a fixed reference point and a defensible entrance. They love the narrow-squeezy-entrance-opening-into-larger-cavern type, because a body that compresses to 8–10 cm diameter has access to architecture that nothing large enough to eat them can follow through. Air-pocket grottos, sealed rock chambers where trapped gas creates a breathable headspace above the waterline, are prized infrastructure. Rare, useful for air-breathing practice, vocalization without predation, and just plain fun to float around in.

Territory is communal. Tool caches, Glowspiral clusters, cleaning stations, and food stores all belong to whoever's there. Maintenance is biological tending: feed the Glowspirals their algae, check on the Forestfish pest control, visit the Crunchcleaner station for a parasite scrub, clear debris from tidal shifts. The den lives because its residents keep it alive.

Radical hospitality governs all of it. Any Osminoki who swims by gets housed and harbored, no questions, no cost. The ocean is trying to eat everyone equally and the Osminoki know it. Turning someone away is functionally a death sentence and culturally unthinkable. The ocean giveth, the ocean taketh, and everyone currently alive is temporarily in the "giveth" column.

Reef-adjacent living is the densest and most desirable, but large gatherings attract predators, so communities stay careful about how tightly they cluster and always camouflage responsibly. The Abbey's ~200 residents represent the practical upper limit of concentrated Osminoki habitation, and even that requires constant vigilance, dedicated watch-swimmers, and the Abbey's trench-wall architecture of one giant cave system threaded with passages too narrow for most things with a rigid skeleton to navigate.

### The Average Experience

A malton unit on Osminok starts the way it ends: in the dark, in a den, with half a brain on watch and the other half drifting through whatever Osminoki dream about. They dream vividly, and they broadcast it. A den full of sleeping Osminoki is a slow-motion light show of involuntary color shifts and texture ripples, each body cycling through its own private narrative. It's quite beautiful. It is also an excellent reason to make sure the den entrance is very, very narrow.

The Stir brings the first half fully awake. Tentacles that have been idly tasting the surrounding water for the entire rest period start reporting: pressure dropping slightly as the migration begins, chemical traces shifting as organisms rise through the water column, the faint EM flutter of a billion small things beginning to move. The pre-hunt routine is pragmatic. Eat something small. Check on the Glowspirals. Taste the current for predator signatures. Signal to denmates with a quick chromatophore pulse — *Going out, who's coming?*

The Rising Hunt is the main event. Group hunts in kelp forest communities are coordinated affairs, with Fastfriends deployed as herders, adults flanking and cornering Shimmerfish against the canopy, juveniles watching from safe vantage points and learning the geometry of ambush. The post-hunt share is immediate and generous. Hoarding gets you shunned; sharing gets you tentacle-twined later during the social window. Surplus goes to the den cache for the juveniles and the elderly.

Bright Drift through High Water is the social heart of the malton unit. Prey has settled at its peak height and gone cautious; predators that follow the migration upward are occupied. This is the closest thing to safe that Osminok offers, and the Osminoki use it. Larger communities gather. Stories get told in full Noki — water-jet modulation, chromatophore displays, bioluminescent emphasis, EM undertones carrying emotional content that no translation can fully capture. Juveniles get taught and played with. Texture-sharing and tentacle-twining happen between partners testing compatibility or simply enjoying contact. Tools get made, caches get restocked, and disputes get settled by whoever can put on the flashier threat display.

The Falling Hunt runs smaller and more opportunistic. A pair of adults with a Fastfriend or two, picking off whatever the descending migration pushes past their territory. Solo hunters work the edges, snatching Crunchclaws or ambushing a Flatmeat that chose the wrong patch of sediment to hide under. The caloric math still applies: the furnace ran all through High Water's social hour, and the body needs refueling before the long rest.

The Settling is wind-down. Return to territory. Evening snacking. So much snacking. Casual social contact with whoever's nearby. Check the den, check the Glowspirals, check the Forestfish, taste the water one more time for anything alarming. Deep Rest is den time. Half the brain goes down first; the other follows a marbec or so later if nothing has triggered the alarm tentacles. The cycle resets.

This rhythm runs strongest on the Twilight Shelves, where the migration signal arrives loud and clear through 200–1,000 meters of water. Vent garden communes feel a muted version through 3,000 meters and supplement it with their own institutional rhythms and the thermodynamic luxury of warmer water (lower heating bill, more time for socializing). Polar Osminoki run the same basic cycle but compressed and solitary — solo hunts with their Fastfriend, no dedicated social windows, the migration signal barely a whisper, the "gathering" just one tentacled hunter and one endothermic buddy curled together under the ice. The Abbey hears the faintest echo of the migration at 7,000 meters and overlays it with Starsinger tending shifts, Void-Singer Choir practice, archive access, communal meals, and pilgrim intake — more structured than wild life, still very informal.

### Off-World

When the migration signal vanishes entirely, the biological clock loses its anchor. Spacefaring Osminoki have no Shimmerlayer, no pressure waves, no chemical tides marking the passage of a malton unit. The flashing lights of digital clocks mean effectively nothing to them. Most latch onto ship schedules, meal rotations, or whatever external rhythm the vessel provides. Traveling Teuthidan Adherents have the Tending Prayer cycle — feeding the polyp jar, the tentacle gestures, the whispered scripture — which offers at least one fixed point in an otherwise structureless existence.

Gaby's malton units in space are anchored by her tending ritual and her cleaning shifts, the closest things she has to the hunt-rest-hunt-rest rhythm of home. She sleeps in whatever cold, damp corner she can find, treats the entire ship as her territory (crews don't always appreciate this), and grazes from the galley whenever hunger or anxiety drives her there. The rhythm of home is gone. She builds a new one from what she can scrounge up, the way every Osminoki always has.

## Technology & Resources

**Can make:**

- Carved stone tools and structures (basalt, volcanic glass)
- Woven organic materials (kelp fiber, processed tissues)
- Bone/shell tools and containers
- Bioluminescent organism cultivation
- Memory-Stones (EM-encoded information storage)
- Simple mechanical devices (levers, wedges)

**Cannot make:**

- Metals (not much combustion underwater)
- Advanced electronics
- Synthetic materials
- Anything requiring precision manufacturing or combustion

**Trade goods offered:** Memory-Stones (unique tech), bioluminescent organisms (pharmaceutical interest), Voidcoral samples, Crunchtreat crystals, labor, and (*extremely* rarely) Starsinger tissue samples.

**Trade goods needed:** Metal tools, advanced technology, refined materials, knowledge (star charts, alien biology data).

The Osminoki are materially poor compared to any other spacefaring civilization. This is partially why they're desperate to leave and why they're valuable as cheap labor: they have almost nothing and need almost everything.

## Companion Species

The Osminoki keep several species in close mutualistic relationships, each on different terms. Fastfriends are partners: named, adorned with shell-bead jewelry, given first share of every hunt, and trained through personalized EM-pulse commands. In return, they extend an Osminoki's hunting range dramatically, herding prey into ambush and retrieving kills from open water too dangerous to linger in.

Forestfish are tolerated guests who serve as pest control in living spaces, picking off Gillworm larvae and smaller parasites in exchange for consistent feeding. Glowspirals are cultivated like crops, selected for brightness over thousands of generations; they ask only algae and a surface to graze on, and provide the steady light that defines an inhabited space. Crunchcleaners are maintained as infrastructure: communities cultivate cleaning stations on prominent rocks, and every organism in the area gets relief from parasites.

No Osminoki claims to *own* any of these creatures, and the distinction matters to them. Fastfriends choose their partners. Forestfish choose their perches. Glowspirals stay because they're fed. Crunchcleaners stay because the real estate is good and they collect the rent. The Osminoki provide conditions and nutrition; the animals provide themselves. This is a meaningful philosophical point for a species that shares everything freely and finds the concept of property ownership genuinely confusing.

## Art & Aesthetics

Osminoki art is multi-sensory and usually both beautiful and useful at once.

**Sculpture:** Rough stone carvings intentionally designed to be refined by natural currents over time. The artist sets the initial form; the ocean smooths and reveals hidden depths over centuries. The age of a territory can be read by the smoothness of its seamarks. Ancient Abbey sculptures are older than written records.

**Dance (primary performance art):** Silent raves in the deep. Multiple Osminoki gathered at a vent garden, swirling and pulsing with bioluminescence and color changes, tentacles weaving complex patterns, EM fields harmonizing, in complete silence to avoid attracting predators. More immersive and multi-sensory than any human performance.

**Auditory music** is not a big thing (sound travels far and attracts predators), but EM field harmonization, water-jet rhythms, and coordinated color displays create a rhythmic pulse perceived through senses land-dwellers mostly don't have.

**Storytelling** is a major pastime — color, texture, movement, EM modulation, and bioluminescence all woven together into performances that land on senses most species don't have.

**Navigation markers** double as tactile sculptures. Directions reference landmarks by touch: "Swim until you taste the Twin Spirals," "When the Coiled Mother's texture becomes smooth as glass, you've found the ancient hunting grounds."

**Humor:** Osminoki are a surprisingly cheerful people. Pranks and practical jokes are common and always good-natured — hiding in unexpected places, mimicking another's color patterns, rearranging someone's cached tools. When translated out of their native language, however, their humor comes off as incredibly morbid to other species. Most of the subtlety is lost when the audience can't perceive 80% of the joke, which is communicated through rapid color-shifting, texture changes, and sarcastic tentacle wiggling.

**Adornment:** Chromatophores make body paint redundant and clothing unnecessary underwater. Instead, Osminoki favor shells, crystal fragments, and small bones fashioned into jewelry, primarily confined to the upper body since tentacles automatically slip out of bracelets and anklets. Necklaces, drapes, wrist decorations, and occasionally carved masks are the most common forms. Adornment is practical as often as it's decorative: a necklace of Driftfilter crystals is beautiful *and* a portable mineral supplement. Among spacefaring Osminoki, collected trinkets from visited worlds become status markers.

**Reactions to Fire:** Having evolved on a world where combustion is effectively impossible, Osminoki have zero frame of reference for open flame. Most are initially fascinated by the heat and quickly learn through observation (or brief, regrettable contact) that it is *not* safe to touch. They recognize it as a different but equally valid method of producing light — interesting, not terrifying. Spacefaring Osminoki, who've already confronted far stranger phenomena than burning gases, tend to treat fire with casual respect rather than fear.

## Death Practices

**Option 1 — Practical Recycling (most common):** Body left in open water. Scavengers consume it within malton units. Nutrients return to the ecosystem. "The Great Sea takes what it gave."

**Option 2 — Feeding the Starsingers (religious):** Body brought to a Starsinger colony and placed among the polyps. The Starsinger slowly absorbs and incorporates it. The dead become part of a living ship. "Return to the Mother." This is what Abbey members often choose.

**Option 3 — Brine Preservation (rare, for the notable):** Body deliberately placed in a brine pool. Perfectly preserved indefinitely, creating a permanent monument. Reserved for great explorers, Abbey leaders, or those who discovered something important. The only "graves" the Osminoki have; brined bodies are sometimes visited by relatives on dangerous pilgrimages.

## The Starsingers (Biological FTL Ships)

*"Those Whose Song Carried Mother Teuthida To Freedom Amongst The Stars"*

Massive siphonophore-like colonial organisms. Individual polyps specialized for propulsion, life support, navigation, defense. Vacuum-resistant outer membrane. Internal gas bladders for pressure regulation. Feed on stellar radiation, space dust, and cultivated internal ecosystems.

**Evolutionary origin:** Starsinger-ancestors evolved a panic-warp escape reflex in response to something that hunted them from above. When threatened, specialized polyps contract simultaneously, creating a localized spacetime distortion that "pops" the organism (and its contents) through to somewhere else. Originally random jumps within the ocean, but after Teuthida, the Osminoki spent millennia selectively breeding them for larger size, vacuum-resistance, radiation tolerance, and responsiveness to EM signal guidance so they could follow her to the stars.

**Navigation:** An Osminoki "choir" uses coordinated EM field modulation to "sing" directional intent. The Starsinger contracts millions of specialized polyps simultaneously, creating a spacetime rupture. Destination is approximately where aimed, with biological drift of plus-or-minus several light-years. Return trips require precise EM recordings from the original jump.

**Why They're Rare:**

- Mother organisms are ancient and difficult to propagate
- Takes decades to grow a jump-capable Starsinger from spawn
- Requires dozens of Osminoki to crew and care for one
- Jump success rate is maybe 60–70%

**How this differs from hyperspace:** Galactic FTL uses Jump Drives, consumable Warp Cores, and established lanes between fixed Jump Points. Starsingers do none of this. They fold spacetime directly, punching a cavitation bubble from one location to another. The fuel is whatever the Starsinger has been eating. The navigation system is a dozen or so Osminoki harmonizing their EM fields at it until it jumps roughly the right direction and distance. Whether Starsinger jumps produce the same time dilation as hyperspace travel is unknown; the Osminoki don't have clocks precise enough to notice, and no one else has ever been aboard one to keep track.

The few that exist are tended by the Abbey if they aren't out in the black on their cosmic voyages. They're irreplaceable living beings, and every jump risks one. This is the fundamental reason Osminoki rely on other species for transport.

## The Off-World Diaspora

Teuthida's journey occurred approximately 28,000 cycles ago — about 500 stones by the Abbey's own reckoning. Since then, the Osminoki have been very slowly and carefully beginning their spacefaring existence, limited by the scarcity of Starsingers, the difficulty of navigating the megastorms, and their reliance on other species' ships for transport.

**What nobody in the galaxy has figured out yet:** The Osminoki have had biological FTL capability for nearly all of those 28,000 cycles. Galactic civilization has had FTL for perhaps a quarter of that time. The Osminoki are, by a staggering margin, the oldest spacefaring species anyone is likely to encounter — and nobody knows, because they count time in stones, happily clean floors for a living, and their ships are giant lumpy singing jellyfish that land several light-years off target without fail. If asked, an Osminoki would say they've been jumping for "about five hundred stones," which sounds to everyone else like they picked it up last season. Some of those Starsingers, jumping and drifting across deep spacetime with their dedicated crews, may have traveled very far indeed — possibly beyond the swirling arms of this galaxy entirely.

**Off-world population:** Only a few thousand Osminoki are away from their homeworld at any given time. They are extraordinarily rare in galactic terms — rare enough to be a curiosity, sometimes a fascination, occasionally a "collector's item" for the types of people who are comfortable with slavery or indentured servitude. This never works out for the slavers; Osminoki have no concept of forced labor, and they are universally master escape artists (cartilaginous skeleton, chromatophore camouflage, squeeze-through-anything physiology).

**Common occupations:** Custodial and maintenance work is the most frequent profession. They find low-gravity environments easy to navigate and cleaning relaxing. They also excel at caring for creatures in aquariums and vivaria, even when the species are completely unlike anything on Osminok.

**Galactic reputation:** Few stereotypes exist because so few people have met one. Reactions range from indifference to fascination (tentacle-appreciating humanoids tend to lose their composure). They're bad with electronics (too wet), terrible with money (too communal), and even worse with paperwork. They don't really get either part of that particular compound word.

**The fundamental vulnerability:** Osminok is a world so full of death, darkness, and perpetual danger that practically any off-world situation is a massive step up in quality (and quantity) of life. This makes Osminoki distressingly easy to exploit. They'll accept terrible wages, cramped quarters, and degrading work because it still beats being hunted by building-sized predators every moment of their lives. If you give one access to a window, it's almost impossible to peel them away.
